Photographing Particles

This work began with research. For a class assignment I was asked to find an artist working in generative art. That search led me to Tyler Hobbs and his use of flow fields, systems that use Perlin noise to push particles along invisible currents. For the class final I built my own series of generative works using the same underlying principle. The class ended and the work existed only on screen which felt unfinished.

During the same period I was spending time alone with the plotter at Knox, learning what it could do. I brought the sketch with me. The particle system used the cursor as its primary input â€” mouseX and mouseY fed directly into the noise parameters, and a mouse click captured whatever state the system was in at that moment. That interaction turned the process into something closer to photography than drawing: I was moving through a landscape of possible images, waiting for the right moment, then pressing to fix it in place.

The interesting frames were rarely the ones I planned. In fact, I could plan nothing. A rotation or a translation made the system look deliberate for exactly before dissolving back into chaos â€” those were the ones worth keeping. Each print is one of those instants. The plotter made them permanent.

The title is literal, I wasn't drawing particles, rather I was photographing them in motion.
